Our Process

It began with a problem:

 

6 in 10 people all around the world value creativity. 75% of all 10 don’t feel like they’re reaching their creative potential.

How do you alleviate a predicament like that?

We started small. Something simple, like a desktop toy, to keep around while working that would engage the mind.

 

The first idea we had was to implement something like a perpetual motion toy, like the quintessential Newton’s cradle.

 

But weirder. But odder, so that it would motivate excitement and innovation rather than inactivity.

 

Surrealism was the best answer. It’s hard not to wonder about things that don’t make sense. We looked through the paintings of the 1920s, and found a common theme: making the mundane extraordinary by combining them in fascinating ways.

A combination that would be sleek enough to fit on a desktop, and embody the idea of a kinetic sculpture — was a spider and a ferris wheel.

 

Strange, and a little random, but those were the adjectives we were looking for.

We started out with sketches full of ideas. A large planetary gear as the centerpiece for the ferris wheel, with the small inner gears connected to linkages for the legs. String with beads between the legs to model dewy spiderwebs, and to make repetitive sounds like a Newton’s cradle. Shiny acrylic gears for the ferris wheel, and maybe some wood for the chassis and legs as a contrast. We weren’t sure if we could fit eight legs. Maybe it had to become an ant, instead (which was a disappointing thought).

We refined our designs, and thought a bit more about the actual mechanisms. Removed the string, since a reliable sound system didn’t seem feasible there — instead, maybe it could walk and have clacking metal feet. Perhaps the carts could be inlaid into the design of the ring of the planetary gear. We increased the number of planet wheels and fit in four legs per side so it could still be a spider. We thought about adding ball joints between the legs and the chassis.

It was time to model our designs in CAD. The chassis became extraneous — we could use the Klann linkage for the legs, and attach them directly to the planetary gear. The planet wheels, in order to stay in place, had to have a frame, so we designed a web-like part. We searched through models of screws for the legs and two of the gears, d shafts for the leg-attached planet wheels, and motors that could power the entire thing with a screw hub that would fit it.

Our first full print was in an ugly, bright yellow acrylic — definitely a draft color — and it became clear that movement wasn’t going to work with the legs and the gears. We shifted to a design that didn’t walk from place to place, and ran through ideas for a motor stand. The legs had to be projected outwards to account for all the metal fasteners in between. (Also, they did not look great in wood.)

It was hard to figure out color, when there were so many possibilities. We could make it wooden and rustic, mirrored and ornate, gold and brilliant, or even red and blue, like Spiderman. Eventually, we decided to make it out of a matte black acrylic, as enigmatic as its very design, with red planet wheels like the markings of a black widow spider.

In the end, the motor didn’t end up being powerful enough to turn the planet gears as well as the leg linkages, and the legs ended up interfering with each other occasionally. We ran out of time to model carts that would work with our design. But it was nearly a fully functioning prototype.

Many parts of our early designs didn’t work. Some parts still don’t. But the process of brainstorming ideas to solve a problem, discarding the unnecessary and keeping the constructive is a process that does work.

 

And we’ve definitely come out of it a little more creative.
